DysRefine: Dysarthric Speech Correction using Tandem HMM-LLM

Anonymous ACL submission
Abstract001
DYSREFINE is a hybrid framework designed002
for dysarthric speech post-ASR corrections.003
We used the Hidden Markov Model (HMM)004
with language models containing fewer than 3B005
parameters, where the HMM serves as the pri-006
mary corrector by modelling token-level ASR007
confusions. At the same time, the LLM pro-008
vides contextual correction for context-level er-009
rors. Proposed confidence-gating mechanisms010
and hybrid selection of the DYSREFINE pre-011
serve reliable corrections by avoiding unneces-012
sary rewriting at both the token and context lev-013
els. DYSREFINE achieves 5.80% WER on the014
combined TORGO and UA-Speech test set, and015
the Llama-3.2 configuration reaching 5.61%.016
In zero-shot out-of-domain, DYSREFINE ob-017
tains 12.90% WER on the in-house Dysarthric018
English corpus, showing that the combination019
of the HMM-lightweight contextual LMs effec-020
tively improves the dysarthric post-ASR refine-021
ment. An interactive demo is available at this022
website.023

4 Experiments 349
Experiments are performed on two standard 350
dysarthric speech datasets, TORGO (Rudzicz et al., 351
2012) and UA-Speech (Kim et al., 2008), and 352
an in-house English dysarthric corpus for out-of- 353
distribution evaluation (OOD). Three open-source 354
LLMs were used to evaluate the effectiveness of 355
LLM-based post-ASR correction for dysarthric 356
speech. The selected models include two decoder- 357
only instruction-tuned LLMs and one byte-level 358
encoderвАУdecoder model. Specifically, we use 359
QWEN 2.5-I NSTRUCT (Hui et al.), LLAMA -3.2 360
(Touvron et al., 2023), andBYT5 (Xue et al., 2022). 361
These models were selected to compare different 362
architectural settings, including dense decoder-only 363
generation and byte-level sequence-to-sequence 364
correction. 365
The primary model used in our experiments is 366
QWEN 2.5-I NSTRUCT (Hui et al.), which is fine- 367
tuned using LoRA-based parameter-efficient train- 368
ing. This model consists of 36 transformer decoder 369
layers, a hidden size of 3584, 16 attention heads, 370
and a context length of 32k tokens. We use this 371
model as the main post-ASR correction system be- 372
cause of its strong instruction-following ability and 373
relatively efficient parameter size. 374
For comparison, we also evaluate LLAMA -3.2 375
(3B; (Touvron et al., 2023)) as adecoder-only LLM 376
to analyze the effect of a larger generative architec- 377
ture on the ASR correction task. In addition, BYT5 378
(Xue et al., 2022) is used as a byte-level encoderвАУ 379
decoder model to examine whether byte-level text 380
modeling can better handle raw-ASR hypothesis 381
outputs, spelling variations, and word-level distor- 382
tions commonly observed in DASR. 383
All models are fine-tuned on paired raw ASR hy- 384
potheses and reference dysarthric transcripts. The 385
ASR hypothesis serves as the input, while the ref- 386
erence transcript represents the intended corrected 387
transcription. Model training is performed using 388
LoRA with cross-entropy loss (Park, 2026). The 389
dataset was divided into speaker-independent train- 390
ing, validation, and test subsets at 70%/10%/20% 391
split, respectively. Each subset maintains a bal- 392
anced distribution of word-level and sentence-level 393
samples, as well as penalty labels (0, 0.5, 1). 394
During inference, each model receives the ASR 395
sentence, the HMM candidate, and the available 396
linguistic context as input. The model is prompted 397
to generate the corrected sentence while preserving 398
the intended meaning and modifying only ASR- 399

LLM-corrector for confidence-gated post-ASR cor- 436
rections. It is a decoder-only causal transformer 437
with approximately 3B parameters with tied embed- 438
dings and grouped query attention. Based on the 439
confidence, it performs minimal corrections in low- 440
confidence regions to avoid unnecessary rewriting. 441
Llama-3.2 (3B; (Touvron et al., 2023)) is a 442
decoder-only LLM baseline for contextual post- 443
ASR correction. It is an autoregressive transformer 444
model with about 3B parameters that uses super- 445
vised instruction-tuning, base fine-tuning, and pref- 446
erence alignment. In our framework, it allows for 447
comparison with QWEN 2.5-I NSTRUCT for mini- 448
mal contextual correction of low-confidence ASR 449
outputs. 450
ByT5 (300M; (Xue et al., 2022)) is used as an 451
encoder-decoder model that processes text directly 452
at the raw UTF-8 byte level. Since DASR errors 453
often appear as spelling noise, rare-word substi- 454
tution, and partial-word distortion, the byte-level 455
encoder-decoder model is suitable for fine-grained 456
correction of HMM- and LLM-generated hypothe- 457
ses. 458

5.2 Zero-shot Generalization to Unseen540
Datasets541
Zero-shot generalization is important for dysarthric542
ASR because speech impairments vary substan-543
tially across speakers, severity levels, and recording544
conditions, making it difficult to collect sufficient545
target-domain data for every speaker. To assess this546
capability, we evaluate the correction pipeline on547
an unseen in-house English dysarthric dataset with-548
out additional fine-tuning, with results reported in549
Table 5.550
The raw ASR output achieves 20.43 WER, 14.84551
CER, 20.43 MER, and 36.67 UEM, indicating a552
clear domain shift from the in-domain evaluation553
settings. Upon applying the HMM corrections,554
WER decreases significantly from 20.43 to 12.90,555
and CER decreases from 14.87 to 8.63, while UEM 556
increases from 36.67 to 66.67. This suggests that 557
the HMM generalized effectively by capturing sys- 558
tematic ASR confusion patterns rather than relying 559
on target-domain memorization. However, neural 560
refinement through the LLMs (QWEN 2.5, LLAMA - 561
3.2, and BYT5) introduces marginal gains or even 562
performance degradation in this out-of-domain set- 563
ting. This indicates that the LLM componentвАЩs 564
robustness must be stated cautiously; while statis- 565
tical HMM modeling generalizes steadily across 566
domains, generative LLMs are highly susceptible 567
to over-correction and hallucination under severe 568
domain shifts. 569

The ablation study in Table 6 evaluates confidence 572

While our framework leverages established tech- 583

indicate that the primary driver of performance 588

handedly reduces the raw WER from 17.55 to 7.80. 590

6 Summary and Conclusions606
In this work, we introduced DYSREFINE , a selec-607
tive Tandem HMM-LLM framework for post-ASR608
correction of dysarthric speech. The method re-609
sults show that HMM-based confusion modelling610
effectively resolves token-level errors, while the611
language model manages contextual errors through612
semantic and syntactic recovery. Furthermore, the613
ablation study reveals that standalone confidence614
gating alone is insufficient, whereas hybrid candi-615
date selection improves robustness by balancing616
correction quality with the preservation of reliable617
ASR content. Performance on the OOD corpus618
indicates that the framework generalises effectively619
beyond the low-resource dysarthric training data,620
although domain mismatch remains challenging. In621
short, DYSREFINE provides a practical, parameter-622
efficient approach to improve DASR performance623
without the explicit need to retrain the underlying624
encode-decoder-based acoustic model, while re-625
ducing unnecessary rewriting and overcorrection626
errors.627
Limitations and Future Work : The proposed628
frameworkвАЩs innovation relies heavily on engineer-629
ing integration of existing tools rather than on novel630
mathematical methods. Furthermore, the reported631
improvements over external state-of-the-art base-632
lines are constrained by differing ASR outputs and633
splits, making a direct 1:1 comparison difficult. 634
Finally, the LLM-based correction occasionally de- 635
graded performance on out-of-domain data, high- 636
lighting a vulnerability to domain shifts and hal- 637
lucinations. This assistive post-ASR tool should 638
not yet be considered a standalone clinical tool. In 639
future, we will expand the benchmarking of the ap- 640
proach by using multiple LLMs at different scales 641
to measure trade-offs among accuracy, latency, and 642
robustness to correction errors. 643
Reproducibility and Artifacts : The imple- 644
mentation, model configurations, training, and 645
evaluation scripts will be released at https:// 646
github.com/PostASR/DysRefine. To ensure 647
complete reproducibility while respecting the 648
sensitive nature of raw dysarthric speech, our 649
anonymized repository will host full configu- 650
ration files ( experiment_2_config.json), the 651
MFA alignment scripts, and the exact data-split 652
indexing keys. We also provide comprehensive in- 653
structions and manifest formats for obtaining the 654
original UA-Speech and TORGO datasets directly 655
from their publishers. 656

B Related Work885
Dysarthric ASR: This problem remains challeng-886
ing due to atypical articulation, reduced intelligi-887
bility, abnormal speaking rate, and large speaker-888
dependent acoustic variability. Early dysarthric889
speech resources, such as UA-Speech and TORGO890
have enabled systematic evaluation of ASR systems891
for impaired speech, however, they also reveal that892
DASR errors are often more irregular than those ob-893
served in typical raw-ASR hypotheses (Kim et al.,894
2008; Rudzicz et al., 2012). Current ASR systems895
based on self-supervised speech representations,896
Conformer architectures, and FastConformer-style897
models have improved acoustic modeling capacity;898
however, these models can still produce phoneti-899
cally plausible, but semantically incorrect hypothe-900
ses when articulation is severely distorted (Gulati901
et al., 2020; Rekesh et al., 2023; Aboeitta et al.,902
2025). Thus, a strong ASR backbone alone is insuf-903
ficient for dysarthric speech, motivating correction904
methods that operate after decoding.905
Post-ASR correction has recently emerged as a906
practical alternative to modifying the recognizer907
itself. Instead of retraining the acoustic model,908
post-ASR correction treats the decoded hypothesis909
as the raw ASR hypothesis and applies a second-910
stage correction model. LLM-based post-ASR911
correction has been studied using 1-best hypothe-912
ses, N-best hypotheses, and prompt optimization913
(Li et al., 2024; Sachdev et al., 2025). These ap-914
proaches are effective when ASR errors can be915
handled as a general text-recovery problem. DASR916
is harder to treat in this way because the errors917
are tied to impaired and speaker-specific articu-918
lation (Caballero Morales and Cox, 2009; Rowe919
et al., 2022). In practice, the decoded sentence is920
often only partly wrong. Most of the words may921
already be correct, while a few errors come from922
phonetic distortion in the speakerвАЩs articulation. If923
the entire hypothesis is rewritten by an LLM, it may924
improve the mistaken part but also disturb correct925
parts of the sentence (Ma et al., 2025; Hernandez926
et al., 2026). Thus, we use selective correction:927
reliable spans are kept unchanged, and the LLM928
is used only when the hypothesis shows signs of 929
uncertainty. The model may fix the actual error, 930
but it may also modify correct words while try- 931
ing to improve the sentence fluency (Leng et al., 932
2023). In such cases, passing the full sentence to 933
an LLM can be risky, as the model may unneces- 934
sarily rewrite correct parts of the hypothesis while 935
trying to improve fluency (Hernandez et al., 2026; 936
Tang et al., 2026). Although the model can cor- 937
rect a wrong substitution, it may also rewrite words 938
that were already correct in order to make the sen- 939
tence sound more fluent. To avoid this, we use the 940
HMM output as an initial check to decide whether 941
the ASR hypothesis actually requires correction. 942
The LLM is then applied only in a controlled man- 943
ner, rather than being allowed to rewrite the full 944
sentence freely. 945
Recent work has begun to address post-ASR 946
correction specifically for disordered or dysarthric 947
speech. Wen et al. (2026) study LLM-based post- 948
ASR error correction for disordered speech, show- 949
ing the relevance of LLMs as transcript-level cor- 950
rection modules. Zheng et al. (2026) propose an 951
LLM-agent method for dysarthric post-ASR correc- 952
tion and point out that WER does not always reflect 953
whether the sentence meaning is preserved. Their 954
JudgeвАУEditor framework uses top-k ASR hypothe- 955
ses to keep reliable parts of the output and edit only 956
uncertain regions. Our work takes a more practi- 957
cal setting, where only the 1-best ASR hypothesis 958
is available. In contrast, our work focuses on a 959
simpler and more applicable 1-best correction set- 960
ting. We first apply an HMM-based noisy-channel 961
corrector to model word-level ASR confusions in 962
dysarthric speech, and then refine the intermediate 963
hypothesis using LoRA-adapted LLMs. 964
Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA): Large language 965
models are useful for post-ASR correction because 966
they can look beyond individual words and use the 967
surrounding sentence to choose a more likely cor- 968
rection (Ma et al., 2025; Leng et al., 2023). Still, 969
updating all parameters of an LLM is expensive, 970
and for this task it is usually not required. The 971
model already has general language knowledge, the 972
correction model is trained to capture systematic 973
patterns between raw-ASR hypotheses and the cor- 974
responding intended reference transcripts. LoRA 975
provides a practical way to do this by keeping the 976
original model fixed and training only small low- 977
rank updates, which reduces memory use while 978
retaining the pretrained modelвАЩs linguistic ability 979

Confidence-Gated Hybrid Correction: LLM- 1032
based Post-ASR correction can cause overcorrec- 1033
tion by rewriting words which are already correct. 1034
This is particularly risky for dysarthric speech, 1035
where an ASR hypothesis may contain severe pho- 1036
netic errors alongside reliable spans. Confidence- 1037
aware methods address the problem by using un- 1038
certainty to guide correction use word-level con- 1039
fidence for disordered speech correction, while 1040
Zheng et al. (2026); Hernandez et al. (2026) use an 1041
LLM agent to preserve confident regions and edit 1042
uncertain ones. 1043
Our work follows the same selective-correction 1044
idea, but derives reliability from an HMM -based 1045
correction model rather than solely from ASR confi- 1046
dence scores or top-k hypotheses. The HMM mod- 1047
els word-level raw-ASR-hypothesis-to-reference- 1048
transcript mappings from the training data and 1049
generates an intermediate corrected hypothesis. 1050
We then estimate correction reliability using emis- 1051
sion likelihood, correction frequency, ASR-HMM 1052
agreement, and edit distance. In this framework, 1053
the HMM serves not only as a first-pass corrector 1054
but also as a confidence guide that helps determine 1055
13

---PAGE 14---
whether the LLM should revise or preserve parts1056
of the ASR hypothesis. Therefore, it act as both a1057
conservative first-pass corrector and a confidence1058
source for LLM refinement. This design utilizes1059
the complementary strengths of HMM and LLMs.1060
HMM S are interpretable and effective for frequent1061
word-level confusions, while LLMs handle broader1062
contextual errors. Since either module can fail,1063
we study confidence-gated correction and hybrid1064
HMM-LLM selection, using the HMM to control1065
when LoRA-adapted LLM correction should be1066
applied.1067

The HMM is proposed as a dysarthric-speech- 1272

prior to processing by the QWEN 2.5-I NSTRUCT 1274
