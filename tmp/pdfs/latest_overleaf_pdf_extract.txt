DysRefine: Dysarthric Speech Correction using Tandem HMM-LLM
guided Post-ASR Refinement
Anonymous ACL submission
Abstract001
DYSREFINE is a post-ASR correction frame-002
work for dysarthric speech. It combines an003
HMM, which captures recurring word-level004
ASR confusions, with LoRA-adapted language005
models for sentence-level refinement. In006
the main combined-test QWEN 2.5-I NSTRUCT007
evaluation, the best available LoRA configu-008
ration, with λ = 2 .0 and α = 16 , achieves009
6.49% WER, 5.41% CER, and 6.48% MER010
on the combined TORGO and UASpeech test011
set. The closest new Qwen rerun reaches 6.52%012
WER at λ = 0.5. In a separate context-level013
LoRA sweep, the best LLAMA -3.2-I NSTRUCT014
configuration reaches 5.61% WER. Confidence015
gating and hybrid selection are evaluated as016
postprocess ablation variants rather than as the017
headline result; the best hybrid selector reaches018
7.16% WER. In zero-shot out-of-domain evalu-019
ation, the HMM stage obtains 12.90% WER on020
the in-house English dysarthric speech corpus.021
An interactive demo is available at this website.022

evolved into an important component of language025
technologies, supporting applications such as Assis-026
tive communication tools, captioning, clinical doc-027
umentation, and spoken dialogue systems. Recent028

mal speech. However, their performance remains034
limited for dysarthric speech, where motor speech035
impairments lead to atypical articulation, irregu-036

and the LLM-corrected transcript. The study is085

HMM model learn the DASR error patterns and087
improve the raw ASR output without changing or088
retraining the original ASR system? RQ2: Can089
lightweight LoRA-adapted LLMs improve the cor-090

errors that the HMM model cannot fully correct?092
RQ3: Can confidence gating and hybrid selection093
improve WER, CER, MER, and UEM while avoid-094

4 Experiments 359
Experiments are performed on two standard 360
dysarthric speech datasets, TORGO (Rudzicz et al., 361
2012) and UA-Speech (Kim et al., 2008), and 362
an in-house English dysarthric corpus for out-of- 363
distribution evaluation (OOD). Three open-source 364
LLMs were used to evaluate the effectiveness of 365
LLM-based post-ASR correction for dysarthric 366
speech. The selected models include two decoder- 367
only instruction-tuned LLMs and one byte-level 368
encoder–decoder model. Specifically, we use 369
QWEN 2.5-I NSTRUCT (Hui et al.), LLAMA -3.2 370
(Touvron et al., 2023), andBYT5 (Xue et al., 2022). 371
These models were selected to compare different 372
architectural settings, including dense decoder-only 373
generation and byte-level sequence-to-sequence 374
correction. 375
The primary model used in our experiments is 376
QWEN 2.5-I NSTRUCT (Hui et al.), which is fine- 377
tuned using LoRA-based parameter-efficient train- 378
ing. This model consists of 36 transformer decoder 379
layers, a hidden size of 3584, 16 attention heads, 380
and a context length of 32k tokens. We use this 381
model as the main post-ASR correction system be- 382
cause of its strong instruction-following ability and 383
relatively efficient parameter size. 384
For comparison, we also evaluate LLAMA -3.2 385
(3B; (Touvron et al., 2023)) as adecoder-only LLM 386
to analyze the effect of a larger generative architec- 387
ture on the ASR correction task. In addition, BYT5 388
(Xue et al., 2022) is used as a byte-level encoder– 389
decoder model to examine whether byte-level text 390
modeling can better handle raw-ASR hypothesis 391
outputs, spelling variations, and word-level distor- 392
tions commonly observed in DASR. 393

Configurations423
All experiments were performed on a desktop work-424

Model Configuration. DYSREFINE combines neu- 438
ral ASR models, an HMM-based statistical correc- 439
tor, and LoRA-adapted neural text-correction mod- 440
els. 441
Wav2vec 2.0 (Baevski et al., 2020) and FastCon- 442

in Appendix E. 446
HMM (Rabiner, 1989) is a probabilistic post-ASR 447
correction model that captures token-level ASR 448
confusion patterns between raw-ASR hypotheses 449
and ground-truth transcripts, guided by confidence 450
gating to handle recurring DASR error patterns. 451
Qwen2.5-Instruct (3B; (Hui et al.)) is the default 452
LLM-corrector for confidence-gated post-ASR cor- 453
rections. It is a decoder-only causal transformer 454

5.2 Zero-shot Generalization to Unseen 557
Datasets 558
Zero-shot generalization is important for dysarthric 559
ASR because speech impairments vary substan- 560
tially across speakers, severity levels, and recording 561
conditions, making it difficult to collect sufficient 562
target-domain data for every speaker. To assess this 563
capability, we evaluate the correction pipeline on 564
an unseen in-house English dysarthric dataset with- 565
out additional fine-tuning, with results reported in 566
Table 5. 567
The raw ASR output achieves 20.43 WER, 14.84 568
CER, 20.43 MER, and 36.67 UEM, indicating a 569
clear domain shift from the in-domain evaluation 570
settings. Upon applying the HMM corrections, 571
WER decreases significantly from 20.43 to 12.90, 572
and CER decreases from 14.87 to 8.62, while UEM 573
increases from 36.67 to 66.67. This suggests that 574
the HMM generalized effectively by capturing sys- 575
tematic ASR confusion patterns rather than relying 576
on target-domain memorization. However, neural 577
refinement through the LLMs (QWEN 2.5, LLAMA - 578
3.2, and BYT5) introduces marginal gains or even 579
performance degradation in this out-of-domain set- 580
ting. This indicates that the LLM component’s 581
robustness must be stated cautiously; while statis- 582
tical HMM modeling generalizes steadily across 583
domains, generative LLMs are highly susceptible 584
to over-correction and hallucination under severe 585
domain shifts. 586

5.4 Discussion599
While our framework leverages established tech-600
nologies, combining HMMs with LoRA-adapted601
LLMs, the overall contribution is biased toward602
effective engineering integration rather than stan-603
dalone algorithmic innovation. Our findings clearly604
indicate that the primary driver of performance605
improvement is the HMM module, which single-606
handedly reduces the raw WER from 17.55 to 7.80.607
The LLM error correction provides a much smaller,608
albeit valuable, secondary contextual refinement.609
In order to understand the behaviour of differ-610
ent models in the quantitative results, we conduct611
a qualitative analysis of the representative word612
and context-level correction outputs as shown in613
Table 7. It clarifies how different correction stages614
handle local lexical errors, context-level seman-615
tic recovery, and the preservation of correct ASR616
hypotheses. HMM is effective for local ASR con-617
fusions but remains conservative in context-level618
recovery. QWEN 2.5-I NSTRUCT and LLAMA -3.2619
offer stronger sentence-level reconstruction, while620
BYT5 is better at overall fine-grained token-level621
repair.622
6 Summary and Conclusions623
In this work, we introduced DYSREFINE , a selec-624
tive Tandem HMM-LLM framework for post-ASR625
correction of dysarthric speech. The method re-626
sults show that HMM-based confusion modelling627
effectively resolves token-level errors, while the628
language model manages contextual errors through629
semantic and syntactic recovery. Furthermore, the630
ablation study reveals that standalone confidence631
gating alone is insufficient, whereas hybrid candi-632
date selection improves robustness by balancing633
correction quality with the preservation of reliable634
ASR content. Performance on the OOD corpus635
indicates that the framework generalises effectively 636
beyond the low-resource dysarthric training data, 637
although domain mismatch remains challenging. In 638
short, DYSREFINE provides a practical, parameter- 639
efficient approach to improve DASR performance 640
without the explicit need to retrain the underlying 641
encode-decoder-based acoustic model, while re- 642
ducing unnecessary rewriting and overcorrection 643
errors. 644
Limitations and Future Work : The proposed 645
framework’s innovation relies heavily on engineer- 646
ing integration of existing tools rather than on novel 647
mathematical methods. Furthermore, the reported 648
improvements over external state-of-the-art base- 649
lines are constrained by differing ASR outputs and 650
splits, making a direct 1:1 comparison difficult. 651
Finally, the LLM-based correction occasionally de- 652
graded performance on out-of-domain data, high- 653
lighting a vulnerability to domain shifts and hal- 654
lucinations. This assistive post-ASR tool should 655
not yet be considered a standalone clinical tool. In 656
future, we will expand the benchmarking of the ap- 657
proach by using multiple LLMs at different scales 658
to measure trade-offs among accuracy, latency, and 659
robustness to correction errors. 660
Reproducibility and Artifacts : The imple- 661
mentation, model configurations, training, and 662
evaluation scripts will be released at https:// 663
github.com/PostASR/DysRefine. To ensure 664
complete reproducibility while respecting the 665
sensitive nature of raw dysarthric speech, our 666
anonymized repository will host full configu- 667
ration files ( experiment_2_config.json), the 668
MFA alignment scripts, and the exact data-split 669
indexing keys. We also provide comprehensive in- 670
structions and manifest formats for obtaining the 671
original UA-Speech and TORGO datasets directly 672
from their publishers. 673

B Related Work902
Dysarthric ASR: This problem remains challeng-903
ing due to atypical articulation, reduced intelligi-904
bility, abnormal speaking rate, and large speaker-905
dependent acoustic variability. Early dysarthric906
speech resources, such as UA-Speech and TORGO907
have enabled systematic evaluation of ASR systems908
for impaired speech, however, they also reveal that909
DASR errors are often more irregular than those ob-910
served in typical raw-ASR hypotheses (Kim et al.,911
2008; Rudzicz et al., 2012). Current ASR systems912
based on self-supervised speech representations,913
Conformer architectures, and FastConformer-style914
models have improved acoustic modeling capacity;915
however, these models can still produce phoneti-916
cally plausible, but semantically incorrect hypothe-917
ses when articulation is severely distorted (Gulati918
et al., 2020; Rekesh et al., 2023; Aboeitta et al., 919
2025). Thus, a strong ASR backbone alone is insuf- 920
ficient for dysarthric speech, motivating correction 921
methods that operate after decoding. 922
Post-ASR correction has recently emerged as a 923
practical alternative to modifying the recognizer 924
itself. Instead of retraining the acoustic model, 925
post-ASR correction treats the decoded hypothesis 926
as the raw ASR hypothesis and applies a second- 927
stage correction model. LLM-based post-ASR 928
correction has been studied using 1-best hypothe- 929
ses, N-best hypotheses, and prompt optimization 930
(Li et al., 2024; Sachdev et al., 2025). These ap- 931
proaches are effective when ASR errors can be 932
handled as a general text-recovery problem. DASR 933
is harder to treat in this way because the errors 934
are tied to impaired and speaker-specific articu- 935
lation (Caballero Morales and Cox, 2009; Rowe 936
et al., 2022). In practice, the decoded sentence is 937
often only partly wrong. Most of the words may 938
already be correct, while a few errors come from 939
phonetic distortion in the speaker’s articulation. If 940
the entire hypothesis is rewritten by an LLM, it may 941
improve the mistaken part but also disturb correct 942
parts of the sentence (Ma et al., 2025; Hernandez 943
et al., 2026). Thus, we use selective correction: 944
reliable spans are kept unchanged, and the LLM 945
is used only when the hypothesis shows signs of 946
uncertainty. The model may fix the actual error, 947
but it may also modify correct words while try- 948
ing to improve the sentence fluency (Leng et al., 949
2023). In such cases, passing the full sentence to 950
an LLM can be risky, as the model may unneces- 951
sarily rewrite correct parts of the hypothesis while 952
trying to improve fluency (Hernandez et al., 2026; 953
Tang et al., 2026). Although the model can cor- 954
rect a wrong substitution, it may also rewrite words 955
that were already correct in order to make the sen- 956
tence sound more fluent. To avoid this, we use the 957
HMM output as an initial check to decide whether 958
the ASR hypothesis actually requires correction. 959
The LLM is then applied only in a controlled man- 960
ner, rather than being allowed to rewrite the full 961
sentence freely. 962
Recent work has begun to address post-ASR 963
correction specifically for disordered or dysarthric 964
speech. Wen et al. (2026) study LLM-based post- 965
ASR error correction for disordered speech, show- 966
ing the relevance of LLMs as transcript-level cor- 967
rection modules. Zheng et al. (2026) propose an 968
LLM-agent method for dysarthric post-ASR correc- 969
tion and point out that WER does not always reflect 970

Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA): Large language982
models are useful for post-ASR correction because983
they can look beyond individual words and use the984
surrounding sentence to choose a more likely cor-985
rection (Ma et al., 2025; Leng et al., 2023). Still,986
updating all parameters of an LLM is expensive,987
and for this task it is usually not required. The988
model already has general language knowledge, the989
correction model is trained to capture systematic990

ballero Morales and Cox, 2009; Rowe et al., 2022).1005
Parameter-efficient tuning is therefore a suitable1006

This motivates our comparison of different model1014
families within the same correction framework,1015
rather than assuming that one large LLM will be1016
sufficient.1017
